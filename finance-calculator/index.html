Statistical Analysis
Full descriptive stats on any data set
Enter numbers separated by commas: 10, 20, 15, 30, 25
Calculates count, sum, mean, median, mode, min, max, range, variance, and standard deviation (both population and sample). Standard deviation measures volatility — critical for Sharpe Ratio and risk analysis.
Expected Value
Probability-weighted average outcome across scenarios
EV = Σ (Probability × Outcome)
Probabilities must sum to exactly 1.0 (100%). A positive EV means the strategy is profitable on average over many repetitions. Essential for evaluating trade setups and strategies before committing capital.

Position Sizing
How many units to trade based on your defined risk
Position Size = (Account × Risk%) ÷ |Entry − Stop Loss|
The most important risk management tool. Ensures that if your stop loss is hit you lose exactly your planned amount — no more. Professional traders typically risk 0.5–2% per trade. Consistent position sizing protects your account from a single bad trade.
Risk / Reward Ratio
Evaluate the quality of a trade setup
R:R = |Take Profit − Entry| ÷ |Entry − Stop Loss|
A 2:1 R:R means you aim to make £2 for every £1 risked. You can be profitable with a sub-50% win rate if your R:R is strong. Most professional traders require at least 1.5:1 before entering a trade. Use with the Break-even Win Rate calculator to see the minimum win rate you need.
Drawdown Calculator
Measure loss from peak and recovery needed
Drawdown % = (Trough − Peak) ÷ Peak × 100
Shows how far your account has fallen from its highest point. A −20% drawdown requires a +25% gain just to break even. Large drawdowns are psychologically damaging and statistically hard to recover from. Keep max drawdown below 20% as a guideline.
Break-even Win Rate
Minimum wins needed to stay profitable at a given R:R
Break-even Win Rate = 1 ÷ (1 + R:R Ratio) × 100
At a 2:1 R:R you only need to win 33.3% of trades to break even. This is the power of good R:R — it lets you lose more than you win and still be profitable. Combine with Trade Expectancy for a full strategy evaluation.
P&L Calculator
Profit and loss on a trade — long or short
Long P&L = (Exit − Entry) × Qty  |  Short P&L = (Entry − Exit) × Qty
Calculates the absolute gain or loss and the percentage return on your invested capital. Works for both long (buy first, sell later) and short (sell first, buy later) positions.
Trade Expectancy
Expected profit per £1 risked across your strategy
Expectancy = (Win Rate × Avg Win) − (Loss Rate × Avg Loss)
The single most important metric for evaluating a trading strategy. A positive expectancy means you are profitable over a large sample of trades. For example +£0.50 means on average you earn 50p per pound risked — regardless of any single trade outcome.
Kelly Criterion
Mathematically optimal bet size for maximum long-run growth
f* = (b × p − q) ÷ b    where q = 1 − p
b = win/loss ratio (avg win ÷ avg loss), p = win probability. The result is the fraction of capital to risk per trade for maximum long-run growth. Most professionals use Half-Kelly (f* ÷ 2) to reduce volatility. Never apply full Kelly without a large, reliable sample of at least 100+ trades.
ROI Calculator
Return on investment as a percentage
ROI = (Final − Initial) ÷ Initial × 100
Measures the total percentage return relative to your original investment. Does not account for time — use CAGR for time-adjusted comparisons. Useful for quickly comparing two investments on an absolute basis.
Compound Interest
The power of reinvesting returns over time
A = P × (1 + r/n)^(n×t)
P = principal, r = annual rate (decimal), n = compounds per year, t = years. Compounding is why long-term investing works — your returns generate their own returns. The difference between monthly and annual compounding can be significant over a decade.
Sharpe Ratio
Risk-adjusted return — how much return per unit of risk
Sharpe = (Portfolio Return − Risk-free Rate) ÷ Std Dev of Returns
Benchmarks: <0 = losing vs risk-free, 0–1 = below average, 1–2 = acceptable, 2–3 = good, >3 = excellent. The risk-free rate in the UK is typically approximated using the Bank of England base rate or gilt yield (~4.5% in 2024). Most hedge funds target a Sharpe above 1.5.
CAGR
Compound Annual Growth Rate — smoothed yearly return
CAGR = (Final ÷ Initial) ^ (1 ÷ Years) − 1
The constant annual growth rate that would produce the same final value as the actual investment. Unlike average return, CAGR accounts for compounding and gives a more accurate picture of investment performance over time.
Leverage & Margin
Understand your real exposure and margin requirements
Leverage = Position Value ÷ Margin  |  Margin = Position Value ÷ Leverage
Leverage amplifies both gains and losses. At 10:1 leverage, a 1% move in the underlying = a 10% move on your margin. Always know what % of your account is at risk. Over-leveraging is the single most common cause of blown accounts. Never use more leverage than you can psychologically handle during a drawdown.
